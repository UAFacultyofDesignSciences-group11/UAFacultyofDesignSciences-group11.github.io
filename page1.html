WHO WE ARE


'Two Face' is an artwork made by a team of four interior architecture students at the University of Antwerp. We were instructed to create a multidisciplinary work of art at scale 1:1. We all have a certain background within architecture or interior. Our background was useful for the realisation of the project as some of us already have some experience in manufactering furniture.



















WHAT WE MADE

We made a composition composed of separate reflective elements, each with its own perception or meaning. They are assembled into a visually coherent entirety, and are each attached separately to a construction that forms the focal point of the work. All the reflective objects are connected to the structure with profiles made of aluminum.














The shape

By attaching the mirrors in different degrees to the wall we have created a curved shape. This gives the visitor the feeling of being surrounded by the mirrors and reflective objects. Because of the curved shape the reflections get an extra dimension.

The attachment

Detail sketch of the attachment of the mirrors and other reflective objects to the OSB wall. They are glued to an aluminum profile that is bent into the correct degrees. Afterwards these profiles are connected to the OSB wall with screws.

The effect

The result is an assembly of all kinds of mirrors and reflective objects from everyday life. When the visitor stands in front of the art object, he or she will reflect in different ways. The work invites the visitor to look at himself differently.

CASESTUDY

In this casestudy we place multidisciplinarity in a broad context by taking a closer look at philosophies, work and activities from, among other things,
the art and design world. This allows us to use ideas, material applications, research and working methods and thought patterns that are specific to a (design) discipline usable in our own design practice. We call this a cross-over of creative processes with designs at the interface between disciplines.

CREATIVE IDEA

From the research of different artworks we have come to an agreement that we take reflection as the starting point for our artwork. We would like to translate both aspects of reflection, which can be both symbolic and literal, into our visual art. To reflect a literal reflection, we can use materials such as glass, mirrors, water, oil, light, and so on. We then put these reflective materials together to arrive at one compostition, in which fragments of the body are reflected. As a symbolic reflection we map out a problem that is happening in the world today, such as social media, in which people now look at someone faster than at themselves. We want the spectator to "participate" in the artwork by means of self-reflection.

The inspiration

In order to understand what multidisciplinarity means in our practice, we studied the way in which artists deal with this concept.

Anish Kapoor is a contemporary artist who intrigued us with his works of art, because he focuses on looking. It is remarkable how Kapoor manages to blur the boundaries between reality and imagination and to invite the visitor to look at the artwork in a different, attentive way. In his work Yet Untitled small fragments are shown by means of reflective material, which together form a unique composition.

Another multidisciplinary artist is José Dávila. It is interesting how he deals with different, often contrasting materials. In his works Practical Structures, Tectonic Games or Doubt and Desire and Die Feder und Elefant he uses large-scale reflective elements, which are kept upright by industrial ratchet bands. As such, he creates great dramatic visual and physical effects of height, weight and balance. 

Dávila draws his inspiration from concepts rooted in minimalism and conceptual art, and encourages the viewer to wonder where the artist's hand begins and ends. The rough, unpolished marble countered by the nylon bands demonstrates the dichotomy between naturally occurring materials and the manufactured ones. In his Cut-outs On going he goes through the 'cutting out' of the central subject in documentary photography, forcing the viewer to imagine that, as an act of creativity, that appeals to the common universal memory. This series of works show a great multidisciplinarity.

We have compiled a report (only in Dutch) in which we discuss, among other things, these intriguing works more detailed. We also analyzed some guest lectures by multidisciplinary thinkers, which make us think about combining different techniques, domains or methods.

The idea

These artists inspired us to work on the theme 'reflection' because it is a very versatile concept that can be interpreted both literally and figuratively. We started thinking about the essence of reflection and we came to the conclusion that mirrors are the object in which you can see yourself the best and that has the same meaning for everyone. But the goal we wanted to achieve is that people could also see themselves in a different way than just a literal image of yourself and that's why we chose to use different mirrored objects that everyone uses in their daily lives because people are search without thinking about it. We have assembled these objects in such a way that a different image is created from each distance. Below you can see some photo's of objects we chose for the collection.

The execution

What’s interesting about our project is that it is made out of found or recycled objects. This ensured that our first assignment in this casestudy was the collection of all reflective objects that we encountered on our path. The artwork consists of 70% mirrors in different shapes and sizes, the remaining 30% are found objects that can be found everywhere. These are for example silver parts of a service, but also building materials such as aluminum or even technical equipment such as a mobile phone. From this complete collection, we could draw a composition. Everything that followed afterwards was calculating thinking about a matching structure. We chose a wall and floor construction from OSB boards, beams and industrial pallets. On the wall, the collected objects were placed in a horizontally curved shape at eye level.

This was made possible by the use of aluminum flat profiles that were bent into different degrees and were cut to different lengths. The construction of the floor, which is built up of pallets, on which OSB boards are also attached, serves as a platform on which people have to stand at the correct distance from the work of art. 

To secure the wall to the pallets, a recess is provided in between which the wall can stand upright. This is additionally secured with tension cables from the floor construction to the top of the wall.

In order to attract as many passersby as possible who can participate in our artwork, we searched for the ideal location to place our object. Not only the audience was important in our choice, also the environment and the light were important criteria. To answer these conditions, we searched for a public space within our school that could put our work of art extra in the spotlight. In the end, we opted for the entrance of the building on the Mutsaard Campus. We were convinced that at this location our artwork would stand out and that there would be an interaction with teachers and fellow students.
