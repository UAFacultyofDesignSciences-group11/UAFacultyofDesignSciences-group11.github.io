WHAT WE MADE

We made a composition composed of separate reflective elements, each with its own perception or meaning. They are assembled into a visually coherent whole, and are each attached separately to a construction that forms the focal point of the work. The object is placed in a room, so we design both the front and the back. The whole of reflective objects is connected to the structure by means of rods made of aluminum.

 












By attaching the mirrors in different degrees to the wall we have created a curved shape. This gives the visitor the feeling of being surrounded by the mirrors and reflective objects. Because of the curved shape the reflections get an extra dimension. Because of the curved shape the mirrors and objects reflect in an extra dimension.

Detail sketch of the attachment of the mirrors and objects to the OSB wall. The mirrors and objects are glued to an aluminum profile that is bent into the correct degrees. This is then screwed onto the OSB wall.

CASESTUDY

In this casestudy we place multidisciplinarity in a broad context by taking a closer look at philosophies, work and activities from, among other things,
the art and design world. This allows us to use ideas, material applications, research and working methods and thought patterns that are specific to a (design) discipline usable in our own design practice. We call this a cross-over of creative processes with designs at the interface between disciplines. 

CREATIVE IDEA

From the research of different artworks we have come to an agreement that we take reflection as the starting point for our artwork. We would like to translate both aspects of reflection, which can be both symbolic and literal, into our visual art. To reflect a literal reflection, we can use materials such as glass, mirrors, water, oil, light, and so on. We then put these reflective materials together to arrive at one compostition, in which fragments of the body are reflected. As a symbolic reflection we map out a problem that is happening in the world today, such as social media, in which people now look at someone faster than at themselves. We want the spectator to "participate" in the artwork by means of self-reflection.
